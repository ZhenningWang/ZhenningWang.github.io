&#x20;   <h4>Electronic Transport under Dynamic Compression</h4>



&#x20;   <p>

&#x20;     Electronic transport provides a sensitive probe of changes in the electronic structure of metals under extreme conditions. 

&#x20;     In particular, electronic topological transitions (ETTs), or Lifshitz transitions, involve abrupt changes in the topology 

&#x20;     of the Fermi surface without symmetry breaking, which can strongly affect transport properties. Zinc is a prototypical 

&#x20;     system for studying such transitions because its anisotropic hexagonal structure makes its electronic states highly 

&#x20;     sensitive to pressure. However, experimental evidence of ETTs in Zn under high temperature–pressure conditions has 

&#x20;     remained controversial. My research investigates how electronic transport evolves under dynamic compression, with the 

&#x20;     goal of identifying signatures of Fermi-surface reconstruction in metals.

&#x20;   </p>



&#x20;   <p>

&#x20;     To access extreme pressure–temperature states, we performed shock-compression experiments using a light-gas gun platform. 

&#x20;     Electrical resistivity was measured in situ using a four-probe configuration with a pulsed DC current, allowing 

&#x20;     time-resolved tracking of transport behavior during shock loading. The Zn sample was sandwiched between copper anvils 

&#x20;     and electrically insulated by mica layers to ensure stable measurements. In parallel, longitudinal sound velocity was 

&#x20;     determined using the reverse-impact method with photon Doppler velocimetry (PDV), providing complementary information 

&#x20;     on elastic properties during compression.

&#x20;   </p>



&#x20;   <p>

&#x20;     The sound velocity evolves smoothly up to \~11 GPa, indicating no structural phase transition in this pressure range. 

&#x20;     In contrast, the electrical resistivity exhibits a clear slope change near 14 GPa, followed by a much steeper increase 

&#x20;     at higher pressures. This anomaly is attributed to a pressure-induced electronic topological transition, where 

&#x20;     reconstruction of the Fermi surface modifies carrier effective mass and scattering rates. The results demonstrate 

&#x20;     that dynamic resistivity measurements serve as a sensitive probe of subtle electronic transitions in metals under 

&#x20;     extreme conditions. This work was published as a first-author paper in <em>Physical Review B</em>.

&#x20;   </p>
